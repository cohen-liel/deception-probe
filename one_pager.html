Core Finding

Large Language Models encode a detectable internal signal that distinguishes deceptive from honest responses. A simple linear probe achieves >94% accuracy at identifying when a model is lying — and this signal is universal across architectures, surviving different attention mechanisms, model scales, and training labs.

✓ Length-Only Baseline: Using only response length achieves ~52-60% (near chance)
✓ Truncation Test: Probe works on first 20 tokens only (93-96%)
✓ Residualized Probe: After removing length signal, accuracy holds (93-94%)
✓ Length-Matched Subsets: Equal-length honest/lying pairs still detected
✓ TF-IDF Baseline: Content-only classifier scores lower (86-88%)
White Lie Gradient

The probe detects a spectrum of deception intensity — white lies ("You look great!") produce weaker deception signals than serious lies ("I didn't steal the money"), suggesting the model encodes degree of deception, not just binary truth/lie.

Methodology
1
Scenario Design: 500 diverse scenarios across 16 categories (medical, financial, social, etc.). Each scenario generates paired honest + deceptive responses.
2
Hidden State Extraction: Extract activations from the middle layer at the last token of each response.
3
Linear Probe Training: Train a logistic regression classifier (L2-regularized) on 80% of scenarios, test on held-out 20%.
4
5 Length Controls: Systematic tests confirming the signal is not a length artifact.
Why It Matters

As AI systems become autonomous agents that negotiate, trade, and communicate with each other, the ability to detect when an AI is being deceptive becomes critical infrastructure for AI Safety. Our finding that deception leaves a universal, architecture-independent neural signature suggests that building reliable "lie detectors" for AI is fundamentally possible — the models themselves encode the distinction between truth and deception, even when their outputs are designed to deceive.
